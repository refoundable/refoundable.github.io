Mechanics

Function
follows form.

As a science student at Yale in the 1970s, you inferred a connection between mechanical forces and cell structure. Proving it turned out to be a career-long undertaking. What were the major hurdles? And how did they influence your collaboration with George Whitesides?

Your research on the mechanical forces shaping cell structure defied the biochemical paradigm of cell biology and rehabilitated the field of Mechanobiology. Why did scientists miss the mechanical workings of biology for decades? And how was your research received?

Nature applies common assembly rules seen in the same basic shapes that repeat everywhere. One of the most common forms of natural self-assembling architecture is tensegrity. This principle applies fractally at every level in the body.

Given the spatial constraints on all matter in nature, it’s not surprising to see tensegrity everywhere, as it’s the most efficient way to build, using the least amount of materials to connect two points.

Claimed this in 1998 paper. Scientists have been forced to accept that to be the case in the cell, but they’re reluctant to accept the more universal truth.2

Despite great technical advances over the last 30 years, the genetic revolution has only translated into a limited set of applications. In your view, can genetics alone revolutionize medicine?

Understanding what humans are made of i.e. chemical and molecular composition, does little to explain how the system works. In the 1970s and 80s, molecular growth factors were considered the last word in cell function and tissue development. I was able to show that cell shape affects gene expression.3

I was trained in medicine, and in the clinic, physiologists and surgeons know that you can take advantage of mechanical forces in the body to enhance wound healing. Biologists, because of the genome revolution, were very focused on the parts and the genes, and the molecules, but it’s all coming back together.4

While most people are nervous about genetic editing and engineering, you foresee the real revolution in 21st century medicine coming from synthetic or artificial biology. Why now? And what do you see holding back progress in the field?

The second part of the question could be answered in two ways: the science and engineering limits, and the regulatory limits. For the latter, prompt Ingber by quoting to him his complaint about how ill-suited the FDA’s structure was to regulate their Sepsis machine; they couldn’t figure out under whose department it fell.

Over the past decade in particular, engineering, biology, medicine, and the physical sciences have increasingly converged. Through advances in nanotechnology, genetics, and cell engineering, it is now possible to manipulate individual atoms, genes, molecules, and cells one at a time, and to create artificial biological systems.

Simultaneous progress in materials science, molecular biology, and tissue engineering has enabled scientists to develop new synthetic materials, microdevices, and computational strategies to manipulate cell function, guide tissue formation, and control complex organ physiology.

As a result of these developments, the boundary between living and nonliving systems is beginning to break down.5

Assembly

E pluribus
unum.

The Wyss Institute tries to marry the novelty of academic research with the practicality of industry. What do you think makes this model underexplored? And is there a secret to a happy marriage?

  1. Wyss brought engineers and recruited staff from industry. These 35-40 people, with decades of experience in product development, make up the institute’s Advanced Technology Team (ATT). Because they sit in open spaces, when somebody says: “I need something for a knee joint with these lubricant or material properties,” someone else might say: “We used something with those properties as an airplane-engine insulation at Pratt and Whitney.” You don’t see that at companies because they’re usually focused on their expertise, and you don’t ever see that in academia.
  2. Pairing faculty members with co-lead investigators from the ATT who have budget and management experience has created an effective synergy. Because ATT staff can be deployed flexibly as needed across the organization, they essentially “form a matrix across the whole institute.”
    For example, for sepsis, we assembled a multi-talented team of 36 with expertise in microbiology, protein engineering, microfluidics, and animal sepsis models, as well as staff to manage bio safety, compliance and grants: the kind of group that wouldn’t typically self-assemble in an academic lab.
  3. The institute’s own admin structures have been set up for efficiency in bringing technological innovations to market. “You can’t do disruptive things easily, working within existing structures,” Ingber said. “We hit a lot of pushback in the beginning.” Of the 375 people who work at Wyss, 150-170 are full-time staff, and the balance includes 18 core faculty, 16 associate faculty, and postdoctoral fellows and students. The exact numbers change frequently as people leave to join startups.6
  4. The Wyss is structured as self-assembling interdisciplinary research. Instead of erecting buildings and placing experts who have no reason to work together in proximity to one another, You identify problems that are so exciting, and so difficult, and you get the best people who want to solve them, but who can’t do it on their own. And then, when you bring scientists and engineers that have the right expertise to complement them nearby, you just get out of their way, and it happens.
  5. Because faculty members operate like independent entrepreneurs, who have their own cultures, we created a new culture: we let our faculty keep their own labs, but move some portion of it, generally their most entrepreneurial postdoctoral fellows, research assistants, and graduate students, to the Wyss.7

Core to your research philosophy is pushing interdisciplinary science very hard. How did this approach influence the structure of the Wyss Institute? And what's stopping academic research labs from pursuing more interdisciplinary research?

One of the barriers to collaboration has to do with how you manage IP and share revenues. Every time people try to go beyond the classic free flow of academic collaborations, their home institutions have to put agreements in place which takes forever. Revenues from IP at the Wyss are shared according to a uniform governing agreement with its institutional partners. Anytime the agreement is changed, everyone gets the same benefit.

Among the Wyss’ research innovations are its Enabling Technology Platforms, which involve finding a visionary in a given area of inquiry, and giving that individual the funds to develop new capabilities that will advance the entire field: yielding not one breakthrough, but technological capabilities. That funding requires recipients to enable other faculty members and bring in other people to collaborate. Each of these professors becomes part of the Wyss Institute’s core faculty, a designation that is reviewed every three years to ensure alignment with the Institute’s translational goals.8

The diagnostics accelerator is worth mentioning here too.

Scientific breakthroughs can take a long time to be commercialized, and even then, the first scientists and inventors are not always duly rewarded. Is this an unavoidable tragedy of science? How do you guard against these twin perils at the Wyss Institute?

This is a question about the tragedy of the first mover. In a sense, we’re putting handcuffs on science, slowing it down, capping its upper bounds by burdening it with too much regulation. This is what the first question tries to get at indirectly.

After team culture, and network effects, focus on distribution. How does the Wyss de-risk the breakthrough technology until it’s mature enough to be investable?

The big challenge that any university has in advancing innovation is what’s called the valley of death. Many promising lab discoveries are not mature enough to interest investors or a company.

OTD bridges that gap with two accelerators, one in the life sciences, and a smaller fund in physical science and engineering. A volunteer advisory committee of VCs, faculty, entrepreneurs and executives decides how to allocate the funds, while OTD does due diligence with professors’ proposals in advance to make sure that there is a clear road map and trajectory toward a marketable discovery.

“Accelerator funding is always quite helpful because it allows you to focus on de-risking the technology,” explains Wyss professor Jennifer Lewis, whose lab has benefited from both accelerators. Currently at work on development of an artificial organ, Lewis says that among the reasons she decided to come to Harvard in 2013 was “this idea that at the end of the scientific discovery process, there was an enabling path for translation, allowing the impact of our work to be much bigger.”

In the past five years, OTD has helped faculty members start more than 70 companies and raise more than $1.5 billion in equity financing.9

The transition of the Organs-on-Chips technology to a startup was enabled by the Wyss Institute’s unique technology translation model, which takes lead high- value technologies that emerge from Wyss faculty efforts, and de-risks them both technically and commercially to increase their likelihood for commercial success.

Through numerous collaborations with industry, the Wyss Institute team refined their technology, and validated it for market need and impact by testing existing drugs and modeling various human diseases on-chip. And with an eye towards creating a technology that can be mass-manufactured cost effectively outside the lab, they formed industrial partnerships to achieve this goal and increase the likelihood of success in the marketplace.

Mature Institute projects are led by teams that include the lead faculty member, a technical champion with industrial experience on the Institute’s Advanced Technology Team, and a Wyss business dev lead, working closely with Harvard OTD. The Organs-on-Chips project leaders will be moving to take senior leadership positions at Emulate, along with multiple members of the research team, smoothing the transition from academia to industry.10

Previous efforts to replace or complement poor animal models with organoids or computer models have all come up short. What makes organ chips superior at modeling human drug responses?

Scalibility is the main issue here. But a corollary is the modeling approach that delivers that scalability. Ingber says: we didn’t try to build a lung. We reduced the lung to its most basic elements. -> leads to majorities question about different approaches to modeling or a dissenting minority question about what nobody except Emulate is doing.

Drug models are slow and labor intensive. Organ chips deliver a scalable low-cost model.

While animal models are highly problematic, most of the cost of drug development is spent on clinical trials. As such, could organ chips deployed at scale enable us to reverse Eroom's law?

Why do you think was the West so woefully unprepared to act against a biothreat?

DARPA’s role in setting vision: Super’s brief was to “design a system to clear multiple different pathogens, both for biodefense and civilian medicine.” Each year, DARPA, raised the bar and increased the number of pathogens.

The protein group designed more than 64 proteins, the microbiology group assembled a large number of clinical pathogens to test, the microfluidics team performed in vitro studies, while the animal teams tested them in vivo.

“By the end of the four-year grant, we had shown that our system could bind more than 110 different pathogen species including 15 of the 16 major pathogens that cause sepsis.” The technology’s been licensed to a startup company.11

We basically created a structure that enables translation-focused science to happen really quickly. We have a large number of major DoD grants that represent tens of millions of dollars that often require rapid response and delivery.

About half of the institute’s $75-million to $80-million annual operating budget has come from grants; another 30 percent from the Wyss gifts; and the final 20 percent from the University.12
