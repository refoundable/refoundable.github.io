Rarely Asked Questions

Perspective is everything.

Why do we need to accelerate science and technology?

Every century in recorded history without exception saw at least one catastrophic conflict. Today, we live in a world that can destroy itself many times over. We truly cannot afford another major outbreak of violence.

Although we can debate the specific details ad infinitum, we can’t brush this problem away, or merely come to terms with it. We have to be the first generation to overcome it.

If we define technology as doing more with less, then it’s the only practical way we can effectively create abundance, prosperity, and security to guarantee our peace.

Sadly, a lot of what passes for innovation today is trivial at best, if not downright cynical. Real science and technology breakthroughs exceed their antecedents by orders of magnitude, so that we’d never willingly choose to go back. In a word, real breakthroughs endure.

Don’t all technology companies decline eventually?

Nobody who’s lived through the decline of once-promising technology companies will mince words about the carnage it leaves behind. We shouldn’t excuse the end of once-bright careers or the destruction of capital any more than we should celebrate senility and death.

Still, not every technology company can be saved. If you work for a stagnant firm that isn’t even aware of its decline, or perhaps already accepts it, you should leave now and do something new.

The beginning of a new venture gives you the widest margin of freedom to start a founding that’s never truly finished. A relentless company may not abolish decline, but it can delay it indefinitely.

What makes a visionary founder?

When a technology company stops reinvesting in new science and technology innovation, it begins to expect the future to look a lot like the past. At that point, the operational excellence of professional managers makes them well suited to lead an organization focused on making incremental gains, or looking to sell.

Visionary founders are different as they offer a clear vision of a better future that radically breaks with the past. For this vision to be valuable, it must be hard to reach. To be inspiring, it must be possible to achieve.

Great founders walk this tightrope by lifting the mission above wealth or status. They push themselves and their company beyond the easy answers of business school, beyond reason itself at times, into the rarified air of faith and total devotion, where no hired help can break in, and no grounded competitor can follow.
