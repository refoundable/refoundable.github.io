Why we’re here

Monopoly is
only the beginning.

To meet the challenges ahead in the 21st century, we need relentless technology companies that never stop reinventing the future.

Here’s why

Research

An experiment
gone wrong.

We have two foundational goals:

  1. Accelerate science and technology innovation.
  2. Help build relentless technology companies.

For the better part of the 20th century, working on cutting-edge research in places like Bell Labs, HP Labs, or Xerox Parc was a great way to meet these goals.

The long list of breakthroughs these labs delivered include the first transistor, solar cell, communication satellite, LED display, and cellular phone to name a few. They also helped to launch and sustain the computer revolution, changing the world forever.

Yet despite this incredible track record of invention, the companies that pioneered these technologies never truly capitalized on the enormous value they created.

Too often, nimble startups like Microsoft, Apple, and Intel would come along, find a new way to take the technology to market, and beat the incumbents with astonishing speed.

Soon a new theory of disruption began teaching how the decline of older technology companies was inevitable. People learned to reduce their expectations of larger firms, until they ceased to be the place where the future happens.

Today it doesn't even surprise anyone that large technology companies neglect long-term research.1

Take Silicon Valley monopolies. As Peter Thiel argues, these companies talk a big game about technology while sitting on tens of billions of dollars, forgetting that capital intensive research is what made Silicon Valley possible.

Instead of Bell Labs, we get weather balloons delivering wifi to Africa. Admirable, but the real question is why we can't do both.

If the growing piles of cash are any indication of how little is going on, we can only suspect that the path ahead is fraught with more stagnation, absent a change of course.

  1. It would be nearly scandalous for any large public company today to pursue research on speculative new technologies that won’t be commercialized for 10 to 20 years, despite reaping the fruits of long-term research by more optimistic corporate labs in the past. While we don’t expect this trend to change anytime soon, it's certainly a badly ignored area ripe for rethinking.↩︎

Startup

Large numbers.
Small problems.

In Silicon Valley lore, a never-ending stream of startups, starting small and scaling fast, eventually solves every problem with the world.

Indeed in theory there are no hard limits on what startups can do, but in practice most tend to stay clear of complex problems taking over a decade to solve. Elon Musk's Tesla and SpaceX are the two exceptions that prove the rule.2

In addition, startups may excel at innovation using existing technologies, but they neither replace nor generate enough new inventions. A health tracking app is no substitute for a cancer cure.

As competition intensifies inside Silicon Valley and out, this lopsided innovation model is beginning to show diminishing returns.

Looking ahead, should there be fewer low-hanging fruits to pick, the best technology founders will have to rediscover how to combine invention, innovation, and execution under one roof.

Therefore it's high time we reopened dormant questions on the monopoly of the research university, and how to build inventive technology companies from the ground up.

We can no longer maintain the myth of disruption and wait for a torrent of startups to save us.

Stanford-cum-Y-Combinator is not enough.

  1. Software startups in particular may be getting easier to start than ever, but this should not blind us to the vast scope and scale of other problems we need new technologies to solve in a successful 21st century.↩︎

Relentless

The mission
never stops.

To address the biggest challenges ahead in the 21st century, we believe that founder-led companies, pursuing important missions over a long time horizon, are in the best position to deliver the biggest breakthroughs again and again.

Very unusually, these ventures combine the clarity of vision with the unwavering devotion to their mission and the long-term commitment required to take on what others deem impossible.

They also stand to be greatly rewarded for their gambits. From 1997 through 2017, Apple and Amazon captured 1.7 trillion dollars in market value as the long-term innovations their founders insistently pursued came to fruition.

Indeed if the most valuable technology companies over the long run are not defined by their first breakthrough, then it follows that the founders of new technology ventures face a decisive question:

How do you build a relentless technology company that never stops trying to reinvent the future?

Next Generation

Make way for
the new new thing.

For decades, engineers delivered on the promise of Moore’s Law, doubling the number of transistors on a silicon chip for the same cost every 18 months. This relentless momentum allowed the modern miracle of micro-processors to make its way into every sector of the economy.

It may be argued that the success of Moore's Law was so complete that it made existing technology -- 19th century subways, 20th century rockets, etc. -- vastly better without requiring additional new breakthroughs.

Perhaps it’s no surprise then that most new products could be best described as incremental improvements on the past. The next generation of iPhone may be twice as fast, but is it really twice as revolutionary as the original iPhone?

These improvements may be important, even necessary, but they will never be enough to escape competition and move your company and the world forward.

Often many companies will claim to be working in secret on next-generation technology, only to come out in the end with a predictable disappointment. What could possibly account for this shortfall?

One might begin by considering an elementary fact, often overlooked, that by calling it the next generation, these companies already narrow the definition of the technology to its last generation.3

To make an improvement by an order of magnitude or more, you must leave behind this inter-generational thinking altogether, and find a new way forward.

This is enough to stop most companies in their tracks, as it stands outside the relative ease and certainty of popular trends, so that the advantage goes to the few who are able to carry through to the end.

When Tesla unveiled its prototype Model S in March 2009, three years before it went to production, few car makers used the lead time to ask some tough questions about their future product pipeline.

Their first wake up call came when the Model S debuted to universal acclaim, quickly turning into one of the best-selling luxury sedans around the world, and propelling Tesla’s brand to global preeminence.

Ten years later, most car makers are still trying to catch up to the Model S of the last decade, while Tesla pulls out further ahead, making new inroads.

Next-generation products may pay off over the next year, but a single new breakthrough product will pay off over the next decade.

  1. We don't believe that the next-generation paradigm applies to revolutionary new products as they will often create their own new categories. Google was not the next-generation portal, but the first automated search algorithm. The original iPhone was not the next-generation pocket computer, but the first real smartphone. To dismiss this difference as semantic is a sure way to miss the next big breakthrough.↩︎

Acquisition

High or low,
Visionaries never sell.

If you can’t always stay ahead of the curve, what do you do? One theory has it that you should go out and buy other firms.

Even so, when you’re late to the future, real bargains will be harder to find, and you’re far more likely to overpay. Also, getting odd technology teams to work in synergy together post-acquisition is never as easy as it's made out to be.4

What’s more, most acquisition deals fall through, so there are no guarantees that your bidding will succeed.

When Yahoo! offered to buy Google and Facebook early on for a billion dollars each, it was rebuffed both times, and while it did succeed in buying hip startups like Tumblr and Flickr, they were not enough to save it.

Therefore, founders would do well to remember that relying on acquisitions to keep your company relevant can be dangerous. The best founders don’t sell.

  1. We do believe some acquisitions to be valuable, specially when product synergies are unusually strong and likely to last, or to avoid the prospect of protracted competition, but never as an excuse for a company to stop having bold new ideas of its own.↩︎

Ideas

The inside story.
Retold.

Another management theory suggests letting innovative ideas percolate from the bottom up.

Leave it up to small groups of smart people inside your company, to work out what your company should build next, by running as many experiments as possible. This underlies much of what goes on inside corporate incubators today.

Nevertheless, what this theory manages to miss is that the most revolutionary products often start out as really awful and unpopular ideas.

To succeed, they must survive first contact with better-sounding and more popular ideas inside the company, only to face off against the stubborn resistance that efficiently robs most original ideas of energy before they’re fully formed.

Only direct intervention from a leader who shifts priorities and shows singular dedication to what is right, even when unpopular, will inspire the most talented people in the company to do the same.

So in practice, running a portfolio with dozens of small projects can only distract you from the single biggest breakthrough no one is making.

Competition

Losers break in.
Winners break out.

It's easy to see competition as a shortcut to innovation. All you need to do is copy the best ideas other people have and keep a close race.

The problem with this view is in treating improvement and breakthrough innovation as equals.

Had Apple continued to obsess about Microsoft as it did in the early 1990s, the Mac operating system may have kept improving, but in trying to win that race, Apple might easily have missed the iPhone.

The other extreme, A/B testing and asking users what they want, doesn’t work early on either. The search space is so large that you’ll end up falling back on dumb luck, or more likely, running out of time before finding the answer.

Effective innovators repeatedly identify the unmet needs of their customers, and design entirely new products to meet them. As a bonus, the competition is far less likely to follow you down what seems like an unfashionable path until it’s way too late.

When Apple was looking for a chip to power the original iPhone, Intel didn’t have a mobile processor powerful enough to offer, as it was too focused on protecting its sizable lead in the PC market.

This led Apple to take another look at ARM-based chips, which came a long way since the days of the Newton PDA, and could indeed deliver the dedicated, low-power chip performance needed for the iPhone.

Eventually, when the full scale of the smartphone revolution became clear, Intel would spend the next decade trying to catch up on dedicated mobile chips, while ARM-based processors now power most of the world’s smartphones.

Real innovation escapes the competition.

Breakthrough

So obvious.
So unexpected.

One way to innovate is to invent something completely new. In their heyday, corporate research labs could still make big bets on highly speculative research if it carried the promise of yielding new technologies.

This explains how the scientists and engineers at Xerox Parc were able to hand-build a computer machine in 1973 -- the Alto -- that was at least a decade ahead of its time.

They didn't stop at one machine. At one point, they wired 200 of these computers together to form a single network -- inventing Ethernet along the way -- and got to see first-hand how the world would come to work in the decades to follow.

By working and programming in the future, they argued, you can invent the tools that will become necessary tomorrow, today.

Indeed, they went on to invent the modern graphical user interface, the first object-oriented programming language, desktop publishing, laser printing, and dozens of equally impressive technologies that made possible the personal computer revolution.

Today, this kind of speculative research is seen by most technology companies as a risky long shot that would take far too long to pay off.

Instead, the prevailing approach to R&D is that of a well-diversified portfolio. At any point, they’re running a random number of small experiments, while diligently keeping track of the competition, and taking young startups on fishing expeditions to try to catch the last wave.

It doesn’t have to be this way. Even if advanced research is out of your reach, the biggest commercial breakthroughs often come from the elegant and surprising integration of existing technologies together, in ways previously deemed unlikely.

In 2003, Amazon had a pressing need to provide its project teams with shared computing infrastructure services like databases, storage, and compute.

Never a company to squander a problem, it soon became clear to its leadership that many outside developers had a similar need for reliable, scalable, low-cost computing infrastructure.

By 2006, AWS launched as the first cloud infrastructure service provider, long before this was even a category, let alone a hotly contested one. Over a decade later, AWS remains Amazon’s most profitable business line.

Far from being a remote probability then, it’s more than likely that your company already has what it needs to make its next big breakthrough, without fully realizing it.

The only way to resolve this paradox, in the words of the late Steve Jobs, is to think different.

End Game

The best endings
go on forever.

Were we to recast the present race between the U.S. and China as one between a future dominated by technology or by politics respectively, we wouldn't be so foolish to assume, like the complacent class, that technology somehow still holds the advantage.

The reality is that China, despite its glaring flaws, never lost sight of the decisive questions on which the ultimate fate of the world will be decided.

For technology to win, and the 21st century to succeed, we need more technology companies, but we also need our technology companies to do more.

The life of a successful venture is measured in decades. That's enough time for the people inside to have long and productive careers. We should not accept to spend those careers incrementally.

A single career may be long, but inside a company busy being reborn, no job description lasts forever. Therefore, to reinvent the future, we must begin by rethinking what we work on today.

Some will wonder whether the success of such relentless companies might have a stifling effect on new startups.

In practice, we find the reverse is true. When technology companies with great reach and scale neglect investing in ambitious, capital intensive projects, they cease building the enabling technologies that ultimately lead to new startups. As Thiel puts it, they become bets against the future.

In addition, the challenges that mission-driven companies face are often so unique, that to the extent they resolve to take them head on, they will end up in a place where they don't have to compete with startups at all.

It's given to us to choose whether daring moonshots should belong to an age far beyond our reach, or whether we can reinvent anew how we go about bringing forth the new world to come.


In this hour of decision, we should not take disruption as our guide. There is nothing inevitable about the demise of a truly singular endeavor. The balance hangs on our faith in action.
