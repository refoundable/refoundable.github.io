Why we’re here

Monopoly is
only the beginning.

To meet the challenges ahead in the 21st century, we need relentless technology companies that never stop reinventing the future.

By John Strider |  Refoundable
Here's Why

Introduction

An experiment gone wrong.

We have two foundational goals:

  1. Accelerate science and technology.
  2. Help build relentless technology companies.

For the better part of the 20th century, working on cutting-edge research in places like Bell Labs, HP Labs, or Xerox Parc was a great way to meet these goals.

They also instrumental in kickstarting and sustaining the computer revolution, changing the world forever. And the long list of breakthroughs made in these labs includes the first transistor, solar cell, communication satellite, LED display, and cellular phone, just to name a few.

Yet despite this incredible track record of invention, the companies that pioneered these technologies never truly capitalized on the enormous value they created.

Nimble startups like Intel and Microsoft would come along, find better uses for the technology, and beat the incumbents with astonishing speed.

At the same time, the rise of the modern research university gave nervous executives at large firms the excuse to offload the burden of running advanced research labs, getting rid of a large cost item, and for a while at least, improving returns.

By the end of the century, a new theory of disruption could make a convincing case that the decline of older technology companies was inevitable. Decades of reduced expectations eventually led to real decline.

Today, it doesn’t even surprise anyone that large technology companies neglect long-term research. 1

Take Silicon Valley monopolies. As Peter Thiel has long argued, these companies pay lip service to innovation by funding small side projects while sitting on tens of billions of dollars in cash. Instead of Bell Labs, we get weather balloons beaming wifi to Africa. Admirable, but the real question is why can’t we do both?

Indeed, if the growing piles of cash are any indication, and absent a drastic change of course, we can only suspect that the path ahead is fraught with more stagnation.

  1. It would be inconceivable for a large public technology company today to announce its intention to pursue research on speculative new technologies that won't find commercial use for one or two decades. And while we don’t expect this trend to change anytime soon, it does raise questions on the health of long-term innovation.↩︎

Startup

Large numbers. Small problems.

The story goes in Silicon Valley that a never-ending stream of startups, scaling rapidly from small beginnings, can eventually rid the world of its biggest problems.

Yet, although startups seem capable of anything in theory, in practice, even cavalier founders know to stay clear of complex problems with a lot of moving parts. Tesla and SpaceX are the two exceptions that prove the rule. 2

And while startups can excel at innovation using existing technologies, that alone cannot make up for the growing absence of breakthrough invention and discovery. A health tracking app is no substitute for a cancer cure.

This lopsided innovation model paid off handsomely when Silicon Valley was unique, but now that innovation is diffused globally, it’s beginning to show signs of diminishing returns as the competition intensifies.

Looking ahead, we believe that the best founders will try to escape the competition by rediscovering how to combine invention, innovation, and execution under one roof.

That will often mean looking for new opportunities outside the narrow scope of computer software and the internet, two areas that have been scoured by countless startups over the past two decades.

Therefore, it’s high time we took a fresh look at what it takes to build a research-driven technology company in the 21st century and re‑examined the unquestioned monopoly of the research university.

We can no longer rely on the legend of dorm room disruption and wait for a torrent of startups to save us.

Stanford-cum-Y-Combinator is not enough.

  1. Software startups, in particular, are easier than ever to start, but we should not let this blind us to the vast range of real-world problems we need new technologies to solve in a successful 21st century. We ignore these problems at our own risk.↩︎

Relentless

The mission never stops.

We believe that founder-led companies pursuing difficult and inspiring missions are well-placed to make the most important breakthroughs of the 21st century, and do so repeatedly.

What makes these ventures so unusual is having a clear vision of a radically better future, combined with unwavering devotion to their mission, and the courage to take on the challenges others deem impossible.

They also stand to be greatly rewarded for their gambits. From 1997 through 2017, Apple and Amazon captured 1.7 trillion dollars in market value, as the long-term innovations their founders meticulously planned and executed came to full fruition.

Indeed, when measured over decades, the most valuable technology companies are never determined by their initial breakthroughs, and so every founder of a new technology venture has a decisive question to answer:

How do you build a relentless technology company that never stops reinventing the future?

Next Generation

Make way for the new new thing.

For decades, engineers delivered on the promise of Moore’s Law, doubling the number of transistors on a silicon chip for the same cost every 18 months. This relentless momentum allowed the modern miracle of microprocessors to make its way into every sector of the economy.

The success of Moore’s law was arguably so complete that it extended the useful life of existing technologies, like 19th-century subways or 20th-century rockets, instead of calling on us to make radical breakthroughs sooner. 3

Perhaps it’s no surprise then that most new products could be best described as incremental improvements on the past. The next generation of iPhone may be twice as fast, but is it really twice as revolutionary as the original iPhone?

These improvements may be valuable, even necessary, but they will never be enough to escape competition and move your company and the world forward.

Often, many companies will claim to be working in secret on next-generation technology, only to come out in the end with a predictable disappointment. What could account for this shortfall?

One might perhaps begin by considering the elementary fact that by calling it the next generation, these companies have already confined the definition of the technology to its last generation. 4

To make a radically better improvement, you must leave behind this kind of inter-generational thinking altogether, and find a new way forward.

This is enough to stop most companies in their tracks, as it stands outside the relative ease and certainty of popular trends so that the advantage goes to the few who can carry through to the end.

When Tesla unveiled the first Model S prototype in 2009, three years before taking it to production, few carmakers used the lead time to ask some tough questions about their future product pipeline.

Their first wake up call came when the Model S debuted to universal acclaim, becoming one of the best-selling luxury sedans around the world, and propelling Tesla’s brand to global preeminence.

Ten years later, most carmakers are still trying to catch up to the Model S of the last decade, while Tesla is making new inroads pulling it further ahead.

Next-generation products may pay off over the next year, but a single breakthrough product will pay off over the next decade.

  1. Seen in this light, the thesis that “software is eating the world,” far from affirming the bright future computers are supposedly building, is the most pessimistic articulation possible of the stagnation thesis that progress in the real world has been far too slow.↩︎

  2. We don’t believe that the next-generation paradigm applies to revolutionary products, as they will often create new categories. Google was not the next-generation portal, but the first automated search algorithm. The original iPhone was not the next-generation pocket computer, but the first real smartphone. To dismiss these differences as semantic is a sure way to miss the next big breakthrough.↩︎

Acquisition

High or low, Visionaries never sell.

If you can’t always stay ahead of the curve, what do you do? One theory has it that you should go out and buy other firms.

Even so, when you’re late to the future, real bargains are harder to find, so you’re far more likely to overpay. Also, getting odd technology teams to work in synergy together post-acquisition is never as easy as it’s made out to be. 5

What’s more, most acquisition deals fall through, so there are no guarantees that your bidding will succeed.

When Yahoo! offered a billion dollars to acquire a promising new search engine called Google, and later a promising new social network called Facebook, it was met both times with rejection. And while it did succeed in buying hip startups like Tumblr and Flickr, they were not enough to save it.

Therefore, you would do well to remember that relying on acquisitions to keep your company relevant can be dangerous. The best founders don’t sell.

  1. Acquisitions are valuable when product synergies are unusually strong and likely to last or to avoid the prospect of protracted competition, but they should never turn into an easy excuse for a company to stop having bold new ideas of its own.↩︎

Ideas

The inside story. Retold.

Another management theory suggests letting innovative ideas percolate from the bottom up.

Leave it up to small groups of people inside your company to work out what to build next by running as many experiments as possible. This lean approach underlies much of what goes on inside corporate incubators today.

Nevertheless, what this theory manages to miss is that the most revolutionary products often start as objectionable and easily dismissed ideas.

To succeed, they have to survive first contact with better-sounding and more popular plans inside the company and overcome the stubborn opposition and inertia that rob original concepts of momentum before they mature.

Only direct intervention from a leader willing to turn down popular ideas, and show exceptional dedication to what is right, even in the face of a storm, will inspire the most talented people in the company to do the same.

In practice therefore, running a portfolio with dozens of small projects can only distract you from the single biggest breakthrough no one is making.

Competition

Losers break in. Winners break out.

It’s easy to see competition as a shortcut to innovation. All you need to do is copy the best ideas other people have and keep a close race.

The problem with this view is in treating improvement and breakthrough innovation as equals.

Had Apple continued to obsess about Microsoft as it did in the early 1990s, the Mac operating system would have kept improving, but in focusing on that race, Apple might easily have missed the iPhone.

The other extreme, A/B testing and asking users what they want, doesn’t work early on either. The search space is so big that you’ll end up falling back on dumb luck, or more likely, running out of time before finding the answer.

Effective innovators repeatedly identify the unmet needs of their customers and design entirely new products to meet them. As a bonus, the competition is far less likely to follow you down what seems like an unfashionable path until it’s way too late.

When Apple was looking for a chip to power the original iPhone, Intel didn’t have a mobile processor powerful enough to offer, as it was too focused on protecting its sizable lead in the PC market.

That led Apple to take another look at ARM-based chips, as they’d come a long way since the days of the Newton PDA, and were capable of delivering the dedicated, low-power chip performance needed for the iPhone.

Eventually, when the full scale of the smartphone revolution became clear, Intel spent the next decade trying to catch up on dedicated mobile chips, while ARM-based processors now power most of the world’s smartphones.

Real innovation escapes the competition.

Breakthrough

So obvious. So unexpected.

One way to innovate is to invent something completely new. In their heyday, corporate research labs could still make big bets on highly speculative research if it carried the promise of yielding new technologies.

That explains how the scientists and engineers at Xerox Parc hand-built a computer machine in 1973, the Alto, that was at least a decade ahead of its time. And they didn’t stop at a single machine, eventually building 2,000 to experiment with personal computing at scale.

At one point, they wired 200 Altos together to form the first local area network – inventing Ethernet along the way – and got to see first-hand how the world would be working in the decades to follow.

By working and programming in the future, they argued, you can invent the tools that will become necessary tomorrow, today.

Indeed, they went on to invent the modern graphical user interface, the first object-oriented programming language, desktop publishing, laser printing, and dozens of equally impressive technologies that paved the way for the personal computer revolution.

Today, this kind of speculative research is seen by most technology companies as a risky long shot that would take far too long to pay off.

Instead, the most common approach to R&D is that of a well-diversified portfolio. At any point, they’re running a random number of small experiments, while diligently keeping track of the competition, and taking young startups on fishing expeditions to try to catch the last wave.

It doesn’t have to be this way. Even if advanced research is out of your reach, the biggest commercial breakthroughs often come from the elegant and surprising integration of existing technologies together, in ways previously deemed unlikely.

In 2003, Amazon had a pressing need to provide its project teams with shared computing infrastructure services like databases, storage, and compute.

Never a company to squander a problem, it soon became clear to its leadership that many outside developers had a similar need for reliable, scalable, low-cost computing infrastructure.

By 2006, AWS launched as the first cloud infrastructure service provider, long before this was even a category, let alone a hotly contested one. Over a decade later, AWS remains Amazon’s most profitable business line.

Far from being a remote probability, then, it’s more than likely that your company already has what it needs to make its next big breakthrough, without fully realizing it.

The only thing stopping most companies from resolving this paradox is the courage, in the words of the late Steve Jobs, to think different.

End Game

The best endings go on forever.

Were we to recast the present race between the U.S. and China as a choice between a future respectively dominated by technology or by politics, the stakes alone would push us to reconsider who holds the advantage at every moment.

The reality is that despite its glaring flaws, China never lost sight of the decisive questions on which the ultimate fate of the world hangs.

For technology to win, and the 21st century to succeed, we need more technology companies, but we also need our technology companies to do more.

The life of a successful venture extends for decades. That’s enough time for the people inside to have careers that are long and fruitful. We should not accept to spend those careers doing incremental work.

A single career may be long, but inside a company busy being reborn, no job description lasts forever. Therefore, to reinvent the future, we must begin by rethinking what we work on today.

Some will wonder whether the success of such relentless companies might have a stifling effect on new startups.

In practice, we find the reverse is true. When technology companies neglect to invest in highly speculative research, they cease building the enabling technologies that give way to new startups. As Peter Thiel puts it, “they become bets against the future.”

Besides, the challenges that mission-driven companies face are often so unique, that to the extent they resolve to take them head-on, they will end up in a place where they don’t have to compete with startups at all.

It’s given to us to choose whether daring moonshots should belong to an age far beyond our reach, or whether we can reinvent anew how we bring about the new world to come.

In this hour of decision, we should not take disruption as our guide. For the relentless, the end is by no means inevitable. The balance hangs on our faith in action.
