Stork Prize

$1M to baby
boom town.

Honoring city governments fighting
to end America’s baby bust.

Jan 14, 2023 |  Refoundable

Current trends in the U.S. birth rate are worrying, with recent years seeing the lowest rate on record in our nation’s history.1

A big driver of this trend are cities. While they offer greater economic opportunities for their residents, those generally come at the cost of higher living expenses and lower fertility rates.2

The Stork Prize is an effort to change that. We believe that cities can buck the trend and end America’s baby bust by enacting policies and providing incentives to support family formation.

And to encourage city governments to take on this challenge, we’re announcing an annual prize of $1M for the city making the greatest strides towards reaching the sustainable fertility rate of 2.1 births per woman.

The winning city for 2023 will be announced on Mother’s Day 2024.

The birds

Can I contribute to the Stork Prize?

Yes. The Stork prize is funded by generous private donors. If you are an interested funder, please reach out: fund@storkprize.org

We’d love to offer a larger prize, fund studies on the best policies cities can adopt to encourage family formation, and build open tools to help city governments measure the impact of their programs.

How will the winning city be chosen?

An expert panel of judges will evaluate the top 100 cities in the nation by population size and select the winning city based on 3 criteria:

  1. Improvement rate: Year–on–year change in the fertility rate.
  2. Target rate: Proximity to the goal of 2.1 births per woman.
  3. Policies: The impact and cost-effectiveness of the programs and incentives put in place to support family formation.

Why now? What do you hope to achieve from this?

The baby bust is a silent crisis that gets harder to solve the longer we wait, with consequences that will be felt for decades. So the sonner we get on with ending the bust, the better off we’ll be as a nation.

Borrowing from Silicon Valley, we believe that you optimize for what you measure, and we’d like to see a growing number of cities start to focus on measuring and optmizing for family formation.

What about other developed countries?

With few exceptions, Europe, Japan, and South Korea have it worse than the U.S., making the baby bust a problem across the developed world.3

We think that solving this problem will take more experimentation and willingness to try out new ideas. If you’re an interested funder based in an OECD country, please reach out: fund@storkprize.org
