Introduction

The frontier. Revisited.

Most people attribute the slowdown in R&D productivity to a resource depletion problem though they never really call it that, or graph it that way, but that is what they mean when they say that all the low-hanging fruit has been picked.

The empirical evidence, at least when it comes to pharma, suggests a different story entirely. Specifically it suggests that the problem is one of alchemy, i.e. the approach we take to finding secrets is wrong and the problem is not with the scarcity or depletion of secrets to be found.

Shape of the curves of R&D: Normal Gaussian Curve v. Nothing, Nothing, Nothing, Hockey stick

Most people believe in depletion whereas the truth is that we live in a world overrun by alchemy problems.

This belief can lead to some strange Malthusian results. For instance, if you expect to find less cures for diseases, than it would start to make sense for you to reduce your exposure to globalization to lower the risk of a global pandemic spreading to your population. It might also give credence to “alternative medecine” à la medical marijuana and opoids.

It might have implications for economic policy. We can’t raise the retirement age because we don’t expect to find cures for old age diseases.

Basically resource depletion theory leads you to underinvest and adopt worse alternatives sooner, and so you stop searching for new finds and theory becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. It leads to things like the failed promises and the failure to try.

It’s worth considering the implications of this theory or outlook on the world from different macro perspectives: the political economy result of this theory is redistribution (higher taxes and more welfare), the climate policy result is carbon taxes and vegetarianism (because we can never figure out things like fusion and healthy alternative meats and so on).

And so the end result of a world that believes in resource depletion is not a stable stagnation but an escalation towards a malthusian struggle for resources.

Search

Perspective is everything.

How do the two different approaches impact how you build technoloy and search for secrets?

The lower the output, the more intense you need to search just to stay in the same place. When you’re in an extractive paradigm, you measure two/three things:

  • Marginal output of individual derrick/lab
  • Rate of new derrick/lab setup
  • Overall output

You play this balancing game where you accept that you can’t affect the methods that produce the output, but you can affect how extensively you scale those methods to get more output. Your reaction to falling output is not to change your methods but to do more and more of the same to get the same overall output. It’s a treadmill situation. This explains how you can have intense investment by pharma with declining output.

Paradoxically, you’re also more likely to reduce your investment in R&D when you expect to get less and less returns. We see that this is indeed what happened as pharma companies basically fired all their scientists after years of intense investment that returned nothing.

This intense scaling also leads you to come up with excuses like “this is the best model we have” and run into map-territory problems. You can never admit that, because you do, you admit that you’re a fraud comitting malinvestment. What makes NNT so hard to accept is that it leads them to cease what they’re doing. But in fact we can’t simply stop. And we can’t keep going with crazy methods. What we need is a different approach. Ditch probability for calculus.

By contrast, when you believe you have an alchemy problem, you don’t focus on scaling your existing methods at all. Instead, your focus is entirely on whether you can produce an output that is 10x better on some dimension compared to current methods, and then worry abot scaling that. It’s the difference between going from 0 to 1 and going from 1 to N, where you believe N to be finite. The second leads to crazy intense competition while the first follows the pattern of nothing, nothing, nothing, then discovery!

Scale

Perspective is everything.

The difference in outlook lead to two very different approaches to durability and scaling.

Depletion leads to consolidation (buying out companies), rationing and/or higher prices, pessimism, portfolio of oil derricks/labs, watch total output very clearly.

Discovery leads you to figure out method and scale it. However, two key issues arise around scaling:

  1. Can you actually raise enough money to scale your company from a small discovery outfit into a fully-fledged pharma company?
  2. Is your discovery a one-off hit or can you do it again? Are you a hollywood studio or a technology company?

The two questions end up being very interlinked. If you believe that you’re in a technology company that can produce more than one breakthrough discovery, then it makes sense to scale and grow.

If you believe that you were essentially “lucky,” and you might not have another breakthrough discovery, but wish to focus instead on scaling what you have, then it makes sense to sell the company to a bigger one. And this is essentially what most companies in R&D do. In other words, most companies actually believe in the prevailing paradigm that we live in a resource depletion world, and it really becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy.

Put another way, most companies and most people actually believe or behave as if we live in a world of resource depletion. It’s only the rare and unusual company that doesn’t buy into this and doesn’t sell.

Startup

Perspective is everything.

what mode do you choose as a startup?

The startup equivalent of “depletion” in the search area is the lean methodology. Why? Because you have a search space and you have to scale your search to get to a result and the search has diminishing returns, but what makes it especially bad for startups is that yu’re racing against a finite resource which is your capital.

So in essence you might hit it off and get lucky i.e. find what you’re searching for against all odds, but it’s far more likely that the large search space overwhelms you and you run out of time before you find anything.

The reason this methodology is popular in software is because software is a well-understood problem that suffers from market risk rather than technical risk. You can figure out the technical details but often not the distribution and adoption details.

Whereas in R&D, this approach doesn’t work becuse you actually have technical risk and the way you mitigate technical risk is by taking a different approach.
