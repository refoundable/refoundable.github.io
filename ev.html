Grant Proposal

Relentless

Innovation
is not enough.

To build enduring technology companies, and accelerate science and technology in the 21st century, we need to reinvent the corporate research lab.

Here’s why

Introduction

An experiment
gone wrong.

We have two foundational goals:

  1. Accelerate science and technology innovation.
  2. Help build relentless technology companies.

For the better part of the 20th century, working on cutting-edge research in places like Bell Labs, HP Labs, or Xerox Parc was a great way to meet these goals.

Yet despite a long list of breakthroughs, including the first transistor, solar cell, and cellular phone, the companies that pioneered these inventions never truly capitalized on the enormous value they created.

Too often, nimble startups like Microsoft and Intel would come along, find a new way to take the technology to market, and beat the incumbents with astonishing speed.

Soon a new theory of disruption began teaching how the decline of older technology companies was inevitable. People learned to reduce their expectations of larger firms until they ceased to be the place where the future happens.

At the same time, the rise of the modern research university made it easier for nervous executives to offload the task of doing advanced research on someone else, getting rid of a large cost item, and for a while, even improving returns.

Today it doesn’t even surprise anyone that large technology companies neglect long-term research.

Take Silicon Valley monopolies. As Peter Thiel argues, these companies talk a big game about technology while sitting on tens of billions of dollars, forgetting that capital intensive research is what made Silicon Valley possible.

Instead of Bell Labs, we get weather balloons delivering wifi to Africa. Admirable, but the real question is why we can’t do both.

If the growing piles of cash are any indication of how little is going on, we can only suspect that the path ahead is fraught with more stagnation, absent a drastic change of course.

Startup

Large numbers.
Small problems.

In Silicon Valley lore, a never-ending stream of startups, starting small and scaling fast, eventually solve every problem with the world.

Indeed, in theory, there are no hard bounds on what startups can do, but in practice, most tend to stay clear of complex problems taking over a decade to solve. Elon Musk’s Tesla and SpaceX are the two exceptions that prove the rule.

Also, startups may excel at innovation using existing technologies, but they neither replace nor generate enough new inventions. A health tracking app is no substitute for a cancer cure.

As competition intensifies inside Silicon Valley and out, this lopsided innovation model is beginning to show diminishing returns.

Looking ahead, should there be fewer low-hanging fruits to pick, the best technology founders will have to rediscover how to combine invention, innovation, and execution under one roof.

Therefore, it’s high time we reopened dormant questions on the monopoly of the research university, and how to build inventive technology companies from the ground up.

We can no longer maintain the myth of disruption and wait for a torrent of startups to save us.

Stanford-cum-Y-Combinator is not enough.

Questions

The frontier.
Reopened.

There are three important questions we must undertake to reopen, since they won’t be asked in Silicon Valley or any corporate boardroom, despite being more pressing than ever.

  1. Is corporate research permanently dead, or can we revive it?
  2. How should we rethink our approach to corporate research to avoid repeating the mistakes that led to its decline?
  3. If research alone isn’t enough, how do we build relentless technology companies that never stop trying to reinvent themselves? Or to use another terminology, how do we extend a company’s founding moment indefinitely?

Whatever wording you choose, it may be worth thinking of these questions as the preamble to a new Micro branch of Progress Studies, centering on the firm and its inside structures that give rise to breakthroughs.

The late Steve Jobs put the problem in simpler terms: “I discovered that the best innovation is sometimes the company, the way you organize.”

Synthesis

Perspective
is everything.

Most attempts to investigate these questions so far have been too broad -- Collins, McKinsey -- or too academic -- Arora et al. -- to carry any practical use for founders.

We believe that the best place to start is with technology companies themselves, gauging first how mission-driven founders are approaching the challenge of building companies that endure.

We’ll also look to earlier periods, to recover undervalued visions of corporate research; to new perspectives from areas like biotech and space; and to recent attempts at establishing advanced research labs.

Instead of publishing individual interviews, we’ll start with a broad topic, and interview 2-4 people pushing against its limits to help us formulate a synthesis.

Once that’s complete, we’ll publish an extensive essay that combines synthesis with generous interview excerpts, keeping intact their original stories and unique insights.

With this almost pedagogical approach, we hope to elevate each essay into a standalone reference on its subject, as well as deepening our knowledge of this underexplored field.

While new subjects will emerge with time, the areas we’ll cover should include running research labs inside growing firms, coordinating the research-to-product cycle, retaining scientific talent, organizing technology acquisitions, etc.

Working full-time, our immediate goal is to produce 2-4 pilot essays we can use to refine the format and sign a sponsor to support additional work.

Practically, this amounts to 4-16 interviews, which should cost under $2K to transcribe if run remotely, or around $5-7K with travel. As these low figures suggest, funding is not the real limiting factor, although it’s still necessary. The greater challenge is getting the interviews we need in a reasonable timeframe.

Few are as well-positioned as Emergent Ventures to help, by virtue of the broad base of relationships you’ve cultivated with forward-thinkers, and your bold call for a new field to further our understanding of progress.

Founders

The long view
changes everything.

This series isn’t aimed at large public companies. It focuses on the next generation of founder-led companies like Airbnb, Stripe, Flexport, Anduril, and other technology ventures still emerging.

What unites these companies is having an important sense of mission, a long way to go, and the courage to pursue hard problems that require new technologies to solve. In staying private longer, their founders also have greater control over their long-term heading.

As a result, these companies are teeming with new ideas and keen on exploring long-term questions. By interviewing their people, we’d be reaching them directly, and hopefully getting their help to grow this discussion much larger.

Moreover, by having a public discussion today, we stand to have a disproportionate impact on how these companies build out their R&D infrastructure over the next decade.

End Game

The best endings
go on forever.

Should the present race between the U.S. and China be recast as a choice between a future dominated by technology or by politics respectively, the stakes alone would push us to always reconsider who’s holding the advantage.

For technology to win, and the 21st century to succeed, we need more technology companies, but we also need our technology companies to do more.

For the relentless, disruption is by no means inevitable.

Every great venture is refoundable.
