Frontiers

Innovation
is not enough.

To build enduring technology companies and accelerate science and technology in the 21st century, we need to reinvent the corporate research lab.

By John Strider |  Dec 7, 2019 |  Refoundable
Here's Why

Introduction

An experiment gone wrong.

We have two foundational goals:

  1. Accelerate science and technology.
  2. Help build relentless technology companies.

For the better part of the 20th century, working on cutting-edge research at places like Bell Labs, HP Labs, or Xerox Parc was a great way to meet these goals. Yet today, it doesnt surprise anyone to know that large technology firms have all but abandoned long-term research.

Take Silicon Valley monopolies. As Peter Thiel has long argued, these companies pay lip service to innovation by funding small side projects while sitting on tens of billions of dollars in cash. Instead of Bell Labs, we get weather balloons beaming wifi to Africa. Admirable, but the real question is why cant we do both?

Indeed, far from delivering the cornucopia these companies once promised, if the growing piles of cash are any indication, we can only suspect that the path ahead is fraught with more stagnation.

Startup

Large numbers. Small problems.

The story goes in Silicon Valley that a never-ending stream of startups, scaling rapidly from small beginnings, can eventually take care of the worlds biggest problems.

Yet, although startups seem capable of anything in theory, in practice, even cavalier founders know to stay clear of complex problems with a lot of moving parts. Tesla and SpaceX are the two exceptions that prove the rule.

And while startups can excel at innovation using existing technologies, that alone cannot make up for the growing absence of breakthrough invention and discovery. A health tracking app is no substitute for a cancer cure.

This lopsided innovation model paid off handsomely when Silicon Valley was unique, but now that computer innovation is diffused globally, the model is beginning to show signs of diminishing returns as the competition with Europe, India, Russia, and China, rapidly escalates.

Looking ahead, we believe that the best founders will try to escape the runaway competition by rediscovering how to combine invention, innovation, and execution under one roof.

That will often mean looking for new opportunities outside the narrow scope of computer software and the internet, two areas that have been scoured by countless startups over the past three decades.

Therefore, its high time we took a fresh look at what it takes to build a research-driven technology company in the 21st century and reexamined the unquestioned monopoly of the research university.

We can no longer rely on the legend of dorm room disruption and wait for a torrent of startups to save us.

Stanford-cum-Y-Combinator is not enough.

Questions

The frontier. Revisited.

There are three important questions we cannot delay asking for much longer as we face down intense competition in the decades ahead:

  1. Is U.S. corporate research stalled out?
  2. How should we rethink our approach to research to avoid repeating the mistakes that led to its decades-long decline?
  3. If research alone isnt enough, how do we build relentless companies that never stop reinventing themselves? Or to borrow from Zero to One, how do we extend a companys founding moment indefinitely?

Given the established relationship between R&D and Progress, these questions hint at a new branch of study to examine the micro and macro forces that determine a companys ability to produce and develop new breakthroughs.

The late Steve Jobs put it more simply: I discovered that the best innovation is sometimes the company, the way you organize.

Synthesis

Perspective is everything.

Most attempts to examine these questions so far have been too broad, e.g., Bahcall, MIT, or too academic, e.g. Arora et al. to carry any practical use for founders. 1

Well start off by going back to the basic micro and macro questions facing anyone starting a research-driven venture today:

  1. Team: Are there any eccentric scientists left? How do you find them?
  2. Funding: How do you sustain research on unpopular or long-term problems? What does a reverse Greshams law funding regime look like?
  3. Frontier: Where is the near frontier today? How can a talented young team get there quickly and work on new and meaningful problems?
  4. Complexity: Are we doomed to ever-narrower specialization? How do you reduce complexity in advanced research fields and drastically cut down on the time it takes to start making headway?
  5. Coordination: In a risk-averse culture governed by the precautionary principle, is there any dangerous research left? How do you even get this research out of the lab and into the world?
  6. Scale: Are startups the only way left in our society to do something new? Can larger companies still run effective research projects? Can the U.S. still organize large-scale research or engineering efforts?
  7. Secrets: Is the U.S. falling behind because whole research fields are becoming forbidden? How do we reopen the frontiers now closed?

Starting from these questions, well interview various innovators and pioneers to help us form a practical synthesis on each theme that well publish, alongside the interviews, in a series of essays.

Each essay will give an overview of the history and present challenges of its theme, and use excerpts from our interviews to provide distinct perspectives, colorful examples, and practical advice on dealing with those challenges today.

By taking this extensive, almost pedagogical approach, we hope to lift the entire series into a reference guide for aspiring founders, while deepening our knowledge of these underexplored questions.

Plan

Step into the breach.

The lack of public discussion around the singular questions raised above is, in our view, an opportunity we can seize, with the support of Emergent Ventures, to attract equally exceptional guests.

These should include research-driven startups like OpenAI, Emulate, and Neuralink, outspoken science pioneers like Laura Deming and Aubrey de Grey, and research funding innovators like Breakout Labs.

While, individually, they may not have all the answers, collectively, they offer a unique guide to research-driven ventures that, once published, cannot escape the notice of researchers and technologists, as well as aspiring and existing founders who follow our guests.

Well use the favorable attention, in turn, to strike our first commercial partnership with a sponsor who believes in our mission and can help our audience to launch and grow new research-driven ventures.

Accordingly, our goal for Q1 2020, working full-time, is to interview 1520 research pioneers, publish 45 essays, and sign our first partner. We expect to spend at least 34 weeks in California chasing the interviews that wouldnt happen otherwise, so we put our starter budget at $12K, and believe we can raise 23x that amount once we start publishing.

Mission

Reinventing the future.

As three decades come to a close on the computer software and internet revolution, with record-high profits for Silicon Valley, a few insiders may already sense that something is off with the state of innovation today, but when the profits are this high, those misgivings get nowhere very fast.

Thats why we believe that change will have to come from the outside. And by having an open discussion on the questions that Silicon Valley insiders are not allowed to ask, we hope to accelerate the pace of this change towards a more intensive mode of progress, and ultimately transform how technology companies get built.

For the 21st century to succeed, we need more technology companies, but we also need our technology companies to do more. We can no longer make progress with the easy answers of the past. To find our way forward, we can never stop reinventing the future.

Every great venture is refoundable.
