Grant Proposal

Relentless

Innovation
is not enough.

To build enduring technology companies and accelerate science and technology in the 21st century, we need to reinvent the corporate research lab.

Here’s why

Introduction

An experiment
gone wrong.

We have two foundational goals:

  1. Accelerate science and technology innovation.
  2. Help build relentless technology companies.

For the better part of the 20th century, working on cutting-edge research at places like Bell Labs, HP Labs, or Xerox Parc was a great way to meet these goals.1

Today it doesn’t even surprise anyone that large technology firms have all but abandoned long-term research. Take Silicon Valley monopolies. As Peter Thiel argues, these companies pay lip service to innovation by funding small side projects while sitting on tens of billions of dollars in cash.

Instead of Bell Labs, we have weather balloons beaming wifi to Africa. Admirable, but the real question is why can't we do both?

If the growing piles of cash are any indication, we can only suspect that the path ahead is fraught with more stagnation.

Startup

Large numbers.
Small problems.

In Silicon Valley, the story goes, a never-ending stream of startups, starting small and scaling rapidly, will eventually rid the world of all its problems.

It may be hard in theory to find the limits on what startups can do, but in practice, even cavalier founders know to stay clear of complex problems with many moving parts. Tesla and SpaceX are the two exceptions that prove the rule.

And while startups can excel at innovation using existing technologies, that alone cannot make up for the lack of breakthrough inventions. A health tracking app is no substitute for a cancer cure.

This lopsided innovation model worked handsomely for Silicon Valley when it was still unique, but now that innovation is diffused globally, the model is beginning to show signs of diminishing returns as the competition intensifies.

Looking ahead, we believe that the best founders will try to escape the competition by rediscovering how to combine invention, innovation, and execution under one roof.

That will often mean looking for new opportunities outside the narrow scope of computer software and the internet, two areas that have been scoured by countless startups over the past two decades.

Therefore, it’s high time we took a fresh look at what it takes to build a research-driven technology company in the 21st century and re‑examined the unquestioned monopoly of the research university.

We can no longer rely on the old legend of dorm room disruption and wait for a torrent of startups to save us.

Stanford-cum-Y-Combinator is not enough.

Questions

The frontier.
Reopened.

There are three important questions we cannot delay asking for much longer as we face down intense competition in the decades ahead:

  1. Is corporate research stalled out?
  2. How should we rethink our approach to research to avoid repeating the mistakes that led to its decades-long decline?
  3. If research alone isn’t enough, how do we build relentless companies that never stop reinventing themselves? Or to use another terminology, how do we extend a company’s founding moment indefinitely?

In the context of Progress Studies, these questions hint at a new branch of study to examine the macro and micro forces determining a company’s ability to nurture breakthrough innovation. The late Steve Jobs aptly summarized the challenge: “I discovered that the best innovation is sometimes the company, the way you organize.”

Synthesis

Perspective
is everything.

Most attempts to examine these questions so far have been too broad -- Bahcall, McKinsey, MIT -- or too academic -- Arora et al. -- to carry any practical use for founders.

We'll start off by going back to the basics to explore the very first challenges facing anyone starting a research-driven venture today:2

  1. Team: Are there any eccentric scientists left? How do you find them?
  2. Funding: What does a reverse Gresham's law funding regime look like? How do you sustain research on unpopular or long-term problems?
  3. Complexity: Are we doomed to ever-narrower specialization? How do you reduce the complexity of the problems we're facing, and cut down on the time it takes to start making headway?
  4. Frontier: Where is the near frontier today? How can a young person get there quickly and work on new and meaningful problems?
  5. Coordination: In a risk-averse culture governed by the precautionary principle, is there any dangerous research left? How do you even get this research out of the lab and into the world?
  6. Scale: Are startups the only way left in our society to do something new? Can larger companies still run effective research projects? Can we still organize large-scale research or engineering efforts?
  7. Secrets: Is the U.S. falling behind because whole research fields are becoming forbidden? Are we moving towards banning even more fields? How do we reopen the frontiers now closed?

These are not easy questions. And to help us form a synthesis for each theme, we'll talk to 2‑4 innovators and forward-thinkers in the field, and publish a series of essays that combine excerpts from those interviews with our synthesis, weaving together unique perspectives, colorful stories, and practical advice.

By taking this extensive, almost pedagogical approach, we hope to elevate each essay into a standalone reference on its theme.

Plan

A big fish
in a small pond.

Our goal by March 1st, 2020, working full-time, is to publish 4‑5 essays and sign our first sponsor. Allowing for 3‑4 weeks in San Francisco, we put our starter budget at $10K and expect to raise 2‑3x that amount in sponsorship once we start publishing.

We'll need a good deal of help reaching the innovators and forward-thinkers who would make great guests for this series, and few are as well-placed as Emergent Ventures to provide it. We also believe that showing up in person will help us get the difficult or chance meetings that wouldn't happen otherwise.

Our guests will range from research-driven startups like OpenAI and Neuralink to young and outspoken figures like Laura Deming and Aubrey de Gray to investors like Breakout Labs.

While individually, they may not have all the answers, collectively, they can help us put together a unique and insightful guide to research-driven ventures.

By addressing these questions today, when they are not being discussed, we believe that we can monopolize attention and use it to reach our core audience of aspiring young founders and working professionals in research and technology.

We hope to leverage our unique position and infuential sponsors to land an opportunity to promote the series (much like a book). Our hope is to kickstart a national discussion on this issue. That will put us in a position to do follow-up series that explore our threee questions in more depth.

End Game

The best endings
go on forever.

If the present race between the U.S. and China is recast as a race between a future dominated respectively by technology or by politics, the stakes alone should lead us to track very closely which side holds the advantage at every moment.

For technology to win, and the 21st century to succeed, we need more technology companies, but we also need our technology companies to do more. We can no longer make progress by relying on easy answers. The only viable way forward that leads to peace and prosperity is to never stop reinventing the future.

Every great venture is refoundable.
