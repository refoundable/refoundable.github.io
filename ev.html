Grant Proposal

Relentless

Innovation
is not enough.

To build enduring technology companies and accelerate science and technology in the 21st century, we need to reinvent the corporate research lab.

Here’s why

Introduction

An experiment
gone wrong.

We have two foundational goals:

  1. Accelerate science and technology innovation.
  2. Help build relentless technology companies.

For the better part of the 20th century, working on cutting-edge research at places like Bell Labs, HP Labs, or Xerox Parc was a great way to meet these goals.1

Today it doesn’t even surprise anyone that large technology firms have all but abandoned long-term research. Take Silicon Valley monopolies. As Peter Thiel argues, these companies pay lip service to innovation by funding small side projects while sitting on tens of billions of dollars in cash.

Instead of Bell Labs, we get weather balloons beaming wifi to Africa. Admirable, but the real question is why can't we do both?

If the growing piles of cash are any indication, we can only suspect that the path ahead is fraught with more stagnation.

Startup

Large numbers.
Small problems.

In Silicon Valley legend, a never-ending stream of startups, starting small and scaling rapidly, will eventually solve every problem with the world.

The limits on what startups can do may seem hard to find in theory, but in practice, even cavalier founders tend to stay clear of complex problems with many moving parts. Tesla and SpaceX remain the two exceptions that prove the rule.

And while many startups innovate using existing technologies, that rarely makes up for the dearth of new inventions. A health tracking app is no substitute for a cancer cure.

This lopsided innovation model was profitable for Silicon Valley so long as it was unique. Yet with innovation getting more diffused, the model is beginning to show signs of diminishing returns as competition, both inside and outside Silicon Valley, intensifies.

Looking ahead, we believe that the best founders will try to rediscover how to combine invention, innovation, and execution under one roof in order to escape the competition.

That will most likely mean looking for opportunities outside the narrow scope of computer software and the internet, two areas that have been scoured by countless startups over the past two decades.

Accordingly, it’s high time we took a fresh look at what it takes to build a research-driven technology company in the 21st century, and re‑examined the unquestioned monopoly of the research university.

We're reached a new turning point. We can no longer hang on to the old myth of dorm room disruption, and wait for a torrent of startups to save us.

Stanford-cum-Y-Combinator is not enough.

Questions

The frontier.
Reopened.

There are three important questions we cannot delay asking for much longer as we face down intense competition in the decades ahead:

  1. Is corporate research stalled out?
  2. How should we rethink our approach to research to avoid repeating the mistakes that led to its decades-long decline?
  3. If research alone isn’t enough, how do we build relentless companies that never stop reinventing themselves? Or to use another terminology, how do we extend a company’s founding moment indefinitely?

In the context of Progress Studies, these questions hint at a new branch of study that examines the macro and micro forces affecting a company’s ability to nurture breakthrough innovation. As the late Steve Jobs summarized the challenge: “I discovered that the best innovation is sometimes the company, the way you organize.”

Synthesis

Perspective
is everything.

Most attempts to examine these questions so far have been too broad -- Bahcall, McKinsey, MIT -- or too academic -- Arora et al. -- to carry any practical use for founders.

To start, we’re going back to the basics to explore the first challenges facing anyone starting a research-driven venture today:2

  1. Team: Are there any eccentric scientists left? How do you find them?
  2. Funding: If science funding creates boom and bust cycles in popular research fields, how do you sustain unpopular, anti-cyclical, or long-term research?
  3. Complexity: When it takes a decade to develop specialized expertise in some fields, who can still speak for the whole? And how do you reduce the complexity to dramatically cut down on this time?
  4. Frontier: Where is the near frontier today? How can a young person get to the frontier quickly doing something new?
  5. Coordination: In a risk-averse culture governed by the precautionary principle, is there any dangerous research left? How do you get this sort of research out of the lab and into the world?
  6. R&D: How do you run unpopular or controversial research projects inside a larger company? How do you manage the separation?
  7. Startup: Are research startups fundamentally different? Does any experience transfer from running or investing in other startups?

To help us form a synthesis for each of these themes, we'll talk to 2‑4 innovators and forward-thinkers in each area, and publish a series of essays that try to combine interview excerpts with synthesis, weaving together unique perspectives, colorful stories, and practical advice.

By taking this nearly pedagogical approach, we hope to elevate each essay into a standalone reference on its theme, while deepening our own knowledge in this underexplored field.

Reach

The long view
changes everything.

Our goal for Q1 2020, working full-time, is to produce 4‑5 essays and sign our first sponsor. We put our initial all-remote budget at $5K, and reasonably expect to raise 2‑3x that amount in sponsorship after we start publishing.

Needless to say, Emergent Ventures brings immense value to bring this series to life by providing well-needed intellectual support, and opening doors to innovators and forward-thinkers outside our reach.

We believe that this series will find a sympathetic audience with the next generation of founder-led companies like Airbnb, Stripe, Flexport, Anduril, and similar technology ventures still emerging.

What unites these companies is having an important sense of mission, a long ways to go, and the courage to pursue hard problems. By virtue of staying private longer, their founders also typically have greater control over their long-term heading.

As a result, these companies are teeming with new ideas and keen on exploring long-term questions. We have every reason to reach out to them directly in the course of our research, and hopefully get their help to grow this discussion much larger.

The hope is that by starting a public discussion today, beginning with a core of like-minded companies, we could end up having a disproportionate impact on the strategies these companies consider as they build out their R&D infrastructure over the next decade.

End Game

The best endings
go on forever.

If the present race between the U.S. and China is recast as a race between a future dominated respectively by technology or by politics, the stakes alone should lead us to track very closely who holds the advantage at every moment.

For technology to win, and the 21st century to succeed, we need more technology companies, but we also need our technology companies to do more.

That can only happen once we acknowledge that the answers which served us well in the past are no longer enough, and that we must find a new way forward. Even then, Progress may not inevitably follow, but neither will Decline. The balance hangs on our faith in action.

Every great venture is refoundable.
