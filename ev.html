Grant Proposal

Relentless

Innovation
is not enough.

To build enduring technology companies and accelerate science and technology in the 21st century, we need to reinvent the corporate research lab.

Here’s why

Introduction

An experiment
gone wrong.

We have two foundational goals:

  1. Accelerate science and technology innovation.
  2. Help build relentless technology companies.

For the better part of the 20th century, working on cutting-edge research at places like Bell Labs, HP Labs, or Xerox Parc was a great way to meet these goals.

Yet despite a long list of firsts, including the first transistor, solar cell, and cellular phone, the companies that made the inventions too often failed to profit from the enormous value they created.

Nimble startups like Intel and Microsoft would come along, find better uses for the technology, and beat the incumbents with astonishing speed.

At the same time, the rise of the modern research university gave nervous executives at large firms the excuse to offload the burden of running advanced research labs, getting rid of a large cost item, and for a while at least, improving returns.

By the end of the century, a new theory of disruption could make a convincing case that the decline of older technology companies was inevitable. Decades of reduced expectations eventually led to real decline.

Today it doesn’t even surprise anyone that large technology firms have all but abandoned long-term research. Take Silicon Valley monopolies. As Peter Thiel argues, these companies pay lip service to innovation by funding small side projects while sitting on tens of billions of dollars in cash.

Instead of Bell Labs, we get weather balloons beaming wifi to Africa. Admirable, but the real question is why can't we do both?

If the growing piles of cash are any indication, we can only suspect that the path ahead is fraught with more stagnation.

Startup

Large numbers.
Small problems.

In Silicon Valley legend, a never-ending stream of startups, starting small and scaling rapidly, will eventually solve every problem with the world.

Certainly in theory, the limits on what startups can do may seem hard to find, but in practice, even cavalier founders tend to stay clear of complex problems with many moving parts. Tesla and SpaceX are the two exceptions that prove the rule.

And while many startups can innovate using existing technologies, that almost never makes up for having entirely new inventions. A health tracking app is no substitute for a cancer cure.

This lopsided innovation model could work well and be profitable so long as it was unique to Silicon Valley. However, as innovation gets more diffuse, the model is beginning to show signs of diminishing returns as competition, both inside and outside Silicon Valley, intensifies.

Looking ahead, we believe the best founders will have to rediscover how to combine invention, innovation, and execution under one roof in order to escape the competition.

That will likely mean looking for opportunities outside the narrow scope of computer software and the internet, both areas which have been scoured by countless startups over the past two decades.

Accordingly, it’s high time we took a fresh look at what it takes to build a research-driven technology company in the 21st century, and re‑examined the unquestioned monopoly of the research university.

We’ve reached a point where we can no longer maintain the myth of disruption and wait for a torrent of startups to save us.

Stanford-cum-Y-Combinator is not enough.

Questions

The frontier.
Reopened.

There are three important questions we cannot delay asking for much longer as we face down intense competition in the decades ahead:

  1. Is corporate research stalled out?
  2. How should we rethink our approach to research to avoid repeating the mistakes that led to its decades-long decline?
  3. If research alone isn’t enough, how do we build relentless companies that never stop reinventing themselves? Or to use another terminology, how do we extend a company’s founding moment indefinitely?

In the context of Progress Studies, these questions hint at a new branch of study that examines the macro and micro forces shaping a company’s ability to nurture breakthrough innovation.

As the late Steve Jobs summarized the challenge: “I discovered that the best innovation is sometimes the company, the way you organize.”

Synthesis

Perspective
is everything.

Most attempts to examine these questions so far have been too broad -- McKinsey, Collins, MIT -- or too academic -- Arora et al. -- to carry any practical use for founders.

We’re going back to the basics to explore the questions facing anyone starting a research-driven venture today:

  1. Team: Are there any eccentric scientists left? How do you find them?
  2. Timing: Most science funding follows fashion trends creating booms and busts in popular fields, so how is any unpopular or long-term research getting done?
  3. Complexity: With fields getting more complex and specialized, how can you reduce complexity?
  4. The Garage: Where is the frontier today? How can a young person get to the frontier doing something new?
  5. Coordination: With so many trials and safety checks, how do you get research out of the lab and into the world? Are there novel ways to experiment with sales and feedback and coordination?
  6. Unpopular Ideas: How do you run unpopular or controversial ideas or projects inside a larger company? How do you manage the separation?

For each theme, we'll talk to 2‑4 innovators and forward-thinkers pushing against its limits to help us form a synthesis. Then, we’ll publish an essay that tries to combine synthesis with interview excerpts, weaving together unique perspectives, colorful stories, and practical advice.

By taking this nearly pedagogical approach, we hope to elevate each essay into a standalone reference on its theme.

Our immediate goal, working full-time, is to produce 2‑3 pilot essays we can use to refine the format and sign a sponsor to support additional work.

The greatest contribution Emergent Ventures can make to the success of this series, in addition to intellectual support, is to open doors to people who would otherwise be very difficult to reach.

Reach

The long view
changes everything.

We believe that this series will find a sympathetic audience with the next generation of founder-led companies like Airbnb, Stripe, Flexport, Anduril, and similar technology ventures still emerging.

What unites these companies is having an important sense of mission, a long ways to go, and the courage to pursue hard problems. By staying private longer, their founders also have greater control over their long-term heading.

As a result, these companies are teeming with new ideas and keen on exploring long-term questions. We have every reason to reach out to them directly in the course of our research, and hopefully get their help to grow this discussion much larger.

The hope is that by starting a public discussion today, beginning with a core of like-minded companies, we could end up having a disproportionate impact on the strategies these companies consider as they build out their R&D infrastructure over the next decade.

End Game

The best endings
go on forever.

If the present race between the U.S. and China is recast as a race between a future dominated respectively by technology or by politics, the stakes alone should lead us to track very closely who holds the advantage at every moment.

For technology to win, and the 21st century to succeed, we do need more technology companies, but we also need technology companies to do more.

That can only happen when we engage difficult questions head-on, and have the courage to change course if we find our present answers wanting. Progress may never be inevitable, but neither is decline. The balance hangs on our faith in action.

Every great venture is refoundable.
