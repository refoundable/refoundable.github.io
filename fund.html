In 1971, Folkman wrote a paper suggesting that cancer tumors stimulate the creation of nearby blood vessels to support their growth using a process he called “angiogenesis.” He decided to find a way to cut off that blood supply in order to halt or reverse tumor growth. At the time, most cancer research centered on studying the tumor cells themselves, and Folkman’s focus on blood vessels was widely seen as a waste of time. A full 32 years later, in 2003, Folkman was vindicated when the largest clinical trial on colon cancer patients for an angiogenesis-inhibiting drug was a major success. The drug, called Avastin, was approved by the FDA in 2004. Learn more↩︎
